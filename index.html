After the Silence

Did the Black Death truly reshape European music, or did the songs of a dying continent carry on, strangely untouched? And why can't scholars agree?

01The Pestilence

A catastrophe with no precedent

The numbers are still debated. Older estimates put it at a third to half of Europe's population, but more recent historians have gone higher still. Ole Benedictow argues for over 60 percent, which would be around 52 million people out of roughly 80 million before the plague (Benedictow, 2021). Economic historians who look at city records tend to land closer to 40 percent (Jedwab et al., 2022).

The reason there's such a wide range comes down to the sources being unreliable. Contemporary writers tended to round up and use dramatic figures, and the more detailed records that historians prefer, like tax rolls, death duties, and wills, are more precise but incomplete and varied a lot by region (Benedictow, 2021). So the answer here is really a range rather than a single number, and that uncertainty is kind of central to what this discussion is about.

Whatever the exact toll, how it spread is better documented. It most likely started in Central Asia, then made its way to the Genoese trading post of Caffa on the Black Sea, and then came by ship to Sicily and the Italian ports in late 1347. From there it followed trade and pilgrimage routes into France, the Low Countries, England, and the German lands over the next four years, and almost nowhere in Europe was left untouched (Benedictow, 2021).

How a dying Europe explained itself

What mattered for music wasn't just the dying but how people actually explained it. Medieval writers generally pointed to two causes: the movements of the heavens and the wrath of God. In 1348 the medical faculty of the University of Paris blamed the outbreak on a conjunction of Saturn, Jupiter, and Mars that had supposedly occurred in 1345 and corrupted the air. Others saw it more simply as divine punishment for human sin. Guillaume de Machaut, who was a poet, cleric, and the leading composer of the time, lived through the plague and in 1349 gave the same kind of astrological explanation for a world that had been emptied by the dead (Machaut, 1349).

If the plague came from the stars and from God's anger, then the music written in response to it was aimed at those same powers. That's why the most obvious musical reaction to the Black Death isn't actually found in the concert repertory but rather in music written for prayer.

02The Question

Two truths that can't both be whole

It seems obvious that a disaster this large must have darkened the music of its time, but that assumption is exactly what scholars argue about, and the evidence really split in two opposite directions.

One reading is fairly intuitive, which is that fourteenth century music was saturated with grief, dread, and an obsession with death. The other is more cynical and pointed out that much of the music that survived from the plague years sounds largely unconcerned, as if the disaster were happening somewhere else entirely. Both positions have solid evidence behind them, which is why the debate has never really been settled.

The sections that follow try to make the strongest case for each side, starting with the argument that music darkened, then the argument that it carried on more or less as before, and then looking at the sacred music that responded to the plague most directly.

03Argument A

The case that music darkened

The firmest evidence for a direct musical response is also the rawest. As the plague crossed the Alps, bands of flagellants moved from town to town singing Geisslerlieder, which were plain vernacular call-and-response songs begging God to lift the plague (Salmen, 2001).

The flagellants were a mass penitential movement that grew dramatically in 1348 and 1349. They believed the plague was God's punishment for human sin, so processions of penitents would march between towns and whip themselves publicly as a form of communal atonement and hope to turn aside divine anger. The movement spread through the German lands and the Low Countries until it started to alarm the Church, and Pope Clement VI condemned it in 1349. Their songs were sung as they marched, strophic and in German rather than Latin, and simple enough that crowds could join in. This was collective prayer rather than anything like a performance (Salmen, 2001).

We only know these songs exist because a priest named Hugo Spechtshart of Reutlingen was troubled enough by what he witnessed to write them down in his chronicle of 1349, which was one of the earliest acts of folk song collection in European history. One surviving melody, Maria muoter reinu mait, survived the time and can still be heard today (Salmen, 2001). Here, we can clearly see the connection between the disaster and the music it produced.

A culture saturated with death

Around that core sits a wider preoccupation with death. The memento mori impulse, meaning "remember that you must die," runs through pieces like the dance-song Ad mortem festinamus, which translates as "we hasten toward death" (Salmen, 2001). It also runs through the visual tradition of the danse macabre, in which a skeletal Death leads pope and king, merchant and peasant alike in a single procession to the grave.

However, there is something worth discussing about. Ad mortem festinamus survives in the Llibre Vermell de Montserrat, which dates from around 1399 (Salmen, 2001), and the large danse macabre murals are fifteenth century (Oosterwijk & Knöll, 2011). Both draw on a tradition of meditating on death that predates 1348 by several centuries, which means they reveal a civilization that was already deeply immersed in thoughts of death rather than one that was changed by the plague. They don't establish on their own that the Black Death actually caused any of this. So the strongest version of this argument really rests on the flagellant songs as its hard evidence, with everything else serving more as atmosphere.

04Argument B

The case that music carried on

The skeptical reading begins with a date. The defining stylistic revolution of the century, the Ars Nova, was already well underway before the plague arrived.

The Ars Nova, meaning "new art," was a French movement that took its name from a treatise associated with Philippe de Vitry around 1322 ("Philippe de Vitry," n.d.). Its innovations were technical but far-reaching, introducing a more flexible system of notation that for the first time allowed beats to be divided into two as easily as three, and making possible intricate rhythmic structures like isorhythm, where a repeating rhythmic pattern organises an entire section of music. It was a deliberate break from the older Ars Antiqua of the thirteenth century, and it was new enough that the people living through it genuinely felt it as a rupture.

Around 1324 and 1325, Pope John XXII issued a decree complaining that the new techniques were burying the sacred words and exciting the senses rather than calming the soul ("Philippe de Vitry," n.d.). He singled out the choppy device called hocket, the flood of rapid small notes, and the piling up of extra voices. The crucial point for this argument is the timing. That controversy was already raging in the 1320s, a full generation before 1348. Whatever was transforming fourteenth century music was, in large part, already transforming it before the plague arrived.

The silence of Machaut

Then there is Machaut. He survived the plague, mourned patrons he lost to it, and described it at length in his poetry (Machaut, 1349), yet nothing in his enormous surviving musical output bears any clear textual or musical sign of the disease. If the catastrophe failed to leave a mark on the work of the very composer who wrote about it most vividly, we should be careful about reading it into the music of composers who said nothing about it at all.

But the biggest problem is about evidence itself. As Christopher Macklin (2010) said that the impermanence of performance makes music much harder to study here than literature or painting. A great deal of what was sung in the streets and at deathbeds was never written down, and so their absence from the manuscript record might just be a matter of survival rather than evidence that people didn't respond musically at all. And it affects both ways as it prevents us from confidently saying that music ignored the plague just as much as it prevents us from saying music answered it directly.

Continuity, not rupture

The century's stylistic developments may owe more to long-running trends than to any single event.

A problem of evidence

Survival is uneven and dating uncertain, so reading any one piece as a "response" is harder than it looks.

05The Clearest Trace

The sound of devotion

If the plague left a clear musical signature anywhere, it's in music of supplication, which makes sense given how people understood the disease. Since the plague was seen as a punishment sent by God through the stars, the natural response was to pray to God and the saints directly. The clearest example is the hymn Stella celi extirpavit, a Marian prayer that's basically a straightforward plea for deliverance from the plague (Macklin, 2010).

Christopher Macklin (2010) traces the hymn's history across late medieval Europe and connects it to the Franciscans, the friars who nursed the sick, died in large numbers themselves, and shared the same astrological and medical understanding of disease that the hymn reflects. The text asks the "star" to restrain the very constellations blamed for causing the sickness, so it's essentially folding the medical theory of the time directly into a prayer. It's worth noting this hymn dates from the fifteenth century, which shows that the plague's effect on music continued well beyond the first outbreak in 1348, since the plague kept coming back for generations after.

The saints who guarded against plague

People also focused their devotion on the plague saints. St. Sebastian came first. He was a martyr killed by a hail of arrows, and since arrows had long symbolized plague sent by God, he became seen as a protector against it. St. Roch came later, a pilgrim believed to have survived the plague himself and to have cared for its victims, and he's traditionally shown lifting his robe to reveal a plague sore on his thigh (Chiu, 2017). Their cults led to a body of protective votive music, including pieces like Du Fay's O sancte Sebastiane (Du Fay, n.d.).

Medicine or poison?

Underneath all of this lay a real ambivalence about music itself, which Remi Chiu (2017) reconstructs in detail. The thinking was rooted in Galenic medicine, which held that health depended on keeping the body's humours in balance and that emotions, or what were called the "passions of the soul," could tip that balance either way. Because music moved the passions, it could in theory calm a frightened patient and strengthen the body against disease. But by exactly the same logic, the wrong music stirring the wrong passions could inflame the body and leave it more vulnerable to infection. Music was prescribed as medicine and feared as poison at the same time.

06The Verdict

So, which does the music tell?

So what does the music actually tell us? My answer is that the Black Death did change European music, but not in the way you'd expect. It didn't make the art music of the time darker or push composers toward grief. What changed was what people sang to and what they sang for. The clearest sign of the plague is in music aimed directly at the things people thought caused it: the flagellants' penitential songs, the prayers to the Virgin in Stella celi extirpavit, and the votive music for St. Sebastian and St. Roch. What I can't find is the plague in music that wasn't written in response to it. The secular music of the time sounds untroubled, the Ars Nova style was already developing a generation before 1347, and Guillaume de Machaut, who lived through the plague and wrote about it a lot in his poetry, left no trace of it in his actual music. It is true that the evidence is not concrete, since most of what was sung in the streets and at deathbeds was never written down, so the lack of plague references in secular music might just mean it wasn't recorded, not that people didn't care. In the end, the real disagreement among scholars isn't about the music itself, it's about what we're allowed to conclude from something being missing, and that's actually the more interesting point: the difference between a time that was genuinely quiet and one whose voice we just lost.
